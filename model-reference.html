A spatially-explicit agent-based model of diarrheal disease in Akuse, Ghana

SPRINGS-ABM simulates the spread of Rotavirus and Campylobacter through a synthetic population placed on a realistic map of Akuse, Ghana. Every simulated day, agents travel between home, school, worship, water, and social locations; the two pathogens spread through person-to-person contact, contaminated water, and zoonotic exposure to free-range animals; sick children accumulate illness; and parents decide whether to pay for medical care using a Cumulative Prospect Theory model of loss-averse, probability-distorting decision-making. Care-seeking, in turn, feeds back into household wealth, child health, and the epidemic itself. The model was built to explore how water infrastructure, vaccination, and the cost of healthcare shape the burden of childhood diarrheal disease.

01

Model at a glance

SPRINGS-ABM was developed as part of the SPRINGS project to evaluate the health and economic impact of water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) interventions, vaccination, and healthcare access in Akuse, Ghana. It combines four ingredients that are usually studied separately: a realistic spatial environment, a heterogeneous household population, dual-pathogen epidemiological dynamics, and a behavioral-economic model of care-seeking — all updated together, once per simulated day, so that feedback loops between illness, behavior, and household finances emerge naturally rather than being assumed.

Fig 1.1 — From parameters to a running simulation. Colored boxes correspond to the legend in the sidebar.

Why two pathogens, and why together?

Rotavirus and Campylobacter are both leading causes of childhood diarrheal illness, but they spread through different ecological pathways: Rotavirus is viral and travels mainly through direct contact and contaminated drinking water, while Campylobacter is bacterial and enters largely through contact with free-range poultry and livestock, alongside a household fecal-oral route. Modeling them side-by-side, with independent but co-occurring infection tracks, lets the model separate water-infrastructure-sensitive disease burden from animal-exposure-sensitive burden, and ask how interventions such as improved water access or rotavirus vaccination shift the balance between them. A child can be infected with both pathogens at once — their illnesses are tracked independently, and their combined severity determines how a parent responds.

02

Spatial environment

The simulation takes place on a discretized 100 × 100 grid covering roughly 30 km² around Akuse, Ghana, so that each cell represents an area of about 60 × 51 meters. The environment blends real-world geography with procedural generation, giving the model a spatial structure that is grounded in the actual study site rather than an abstract lattice.

Boundary & residences
A fixed geographic boundary around Akuse is rasterized into the 100×100 grid. Every unoccupied, valid cell inside the boundary is a potential household location; no more than one household may occupy a single cell.
→
Points of interest
Schools and places of worship are placed using real OpenStreetMap locations near Akuse. Water collection points use real coordinates from a field water-sampling survey, each mapped to its closest matching grid cell.
→
Animal density
A continuous layer represents free-range poultry and livestock density — the environmental reservoir that drives zoonotic Campylobacter exposure.

Animal density layer

Because poultry and livestock are a major zoonotic reservoir for Campylobacter, the model needs a spatial proxy for where animals are concentrated. Unlike the water, school, and worship layers — which are baked into the grid file at creation time — the animal density layers are derived at runtime from each simulation run's household ownership draw. Two species-specific layers are built: one for poultry and one for ruminants.

Each household that owns animals of a given species "stamps" an unnormalised 2D Gaussian bump (peak = 1.0 at its own home cell) onto an otherwise-empty raster. Bumps from all owning households are summed and clipped to [0, 1]:

per-species density surface
\[ f_s(x,y) = \text{clip}\!\left(\sum_{h \in \text{owners}_s} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-x_h)^2+(y-y_h)^2}{2\sigma_s^2}\right),\ 0,\ 1\right) \]

where \((x_h, y_h)\) is household \(h\)'s home cell, \(\sigma_s\) is the species-specific roam radius in grid cells (\(\sigma_{poultry}=1.0\), \(\sigma_{ruminant}=2.0\)), and the sum runs over all households that own animals of species \(s\). Because the kernel is unnormalised (peak = 1, not sum = 1), a household's own-cell density is not diluted by the blur — it remains at 1.0 regardless of how many nearby non-owning cells exist. Overlapping bumps from multiple nearby owning households accumulate additively up to the [0, 1] cap, so areas with several owning households nearby reach higher densities than isolated owners.

Household ownership is drawn once per run: each household independently becomes a poultry owner with probability 0.536 and a ruminant owner with probability 0.330, calibrated from a compiled Ghana DHS/MICS-style survey (IDW-interpolated over rural clusters nationwide since no survey cluster falls inside Akuse: effectively a rural-southern-Ghana average). Because ownership and home location both vary with the random seed, the animal density layers differ across runs even when the underlying grid is the same.

Ownership probabilities are calibrated from national rural-survey data rather than Akuse-specific measurements. Species weights and roam sigmas are literature-informed starting points, not fitted values — treat them as sensitivity-analysis parameters and recalibrate human_animal_interaction_rate alongside them.

Water sources: a dynamic layer

Unlike the other layers, water sources change state over the course of the simulation — each one is either clean or contaminated. Two independent mechanisms can contaminate a source: infectious people shedding pathogen while visiting it, and sudden environmental shocks (e.g. heavy-rain runoff) that can contaminate every clean source in the grid at once. Contaminated sources also recover naturally over time. The full water dynamics — and how they connect to Rotavirus transmission — are described in §06.

Water sources are currently treated uniformly (piped supply and open surface water behave identically), and the contamination-shock and recovery probabilities are working assumptions rather than calibrated values. Planned refinements include source-type-specific rates and seasonal forcing (rainy vs. dry season), calibrated against empirical water-quality and infrastructure data from the study region.
03

Population & households

The model initializes a population of individual agents (5,000 by default) grouped into households, the fundamental social and economic unit of the simulation. Households pool their money and share their home location, while every individual has their own health, age, and disease status.

Household formation

Household sizes are drawn from a shifted Poisson distribution, guaranteeing every household has at least one member with a mean size of 3.2 — the reported average for the district containing Akuse. The first member of each household is always an adult. Every subsequent member independently becomes a child under 5 with an 14.5% probability (otherwise another adult) — a rate taken from national census data on the share of children aged five or under in nearby rural localities.

Age & the "parent" role

Children are given a continuous age drawn uniformly between 0 and 60 months; adults are fixed at 30 years. The first adult found in any household that contains at least one child is designated the household's parent — the one agent responsible for that household's care-seeking decisions (§09). The population is closed: there are no births or deaths over the course of a run.

Health & wealth at initialization

Health is a continuous value on \([0,1]\), sampled independently for every agent from a normal distribution (\(\mu=0.6\), \(\sigma=0.2\)) truncated to that range. Wealth, by contrast, is a pooled household resource rather than a personal balance — a single value is drawn once per household (\(\mu=0.3\), \(\sigma=0.15\), truncated to \([0,1]\)) and shared equally by every member, since children have no income of their own and household money is, in practice, a shared resource.

Behavioral personas

Every agent is assigned one of 32 distinct behavioral archetypes, generated in advance using Latin Hypercube sampling across three dimensions: how much weight a person places on wealth versus health, how they distort risk probabilities, and how loss-averse they are. These parameters become consequential specifically for parents, who use them when weighing whether to pay for a sick child's care (§09).

Placement on the map

Each household — not each individual — is assigned one random, unoccupied residential cell; every member of that household shares its coordinates as their home location. From that home, the nearest school, place of worship, and water source are pre-computed once and held fixed for the rest of the run, giving every household a consistent, geographically sensible set of destinations.

Household size, child probability, and the health/wealth distributions are grounded in regional census figures and treated as reasonable working assumptions; the authors note that these should ultimately be aligned with empirical health and wealth proxies specific to the study population.
04

Daily mobility & activity

The simulation runs on a dual-phase daily cycle: during the day phase, agents travel to an activity location; during the night phase, everyone returns home. Because both pathogens spread through co-located contact, where people spend their day is what ultimately drives transmission.

Time-use profiles

At the start of each day, every agent draws one activity from their own personal probability distribution over five options: staying home, going to school, attending worship, collecting water, or a social visit. Children and adults follow structurally different baseline profiles, each with 0–10% random noise added before the probabilities are renormalized to 100%:

Children never collect water, attend worship, or make social visits in this model; adults never attend school. These baseline weights are working assumptions meant to approximate local time-use patterns and are assumed constant — they do not vary by weekday or season.

Social visits are spatial, not networked

Adults assigned to a social visit look for a nearby host: they randomly select someone currently at home within a 5-grid-cell radius and travel to that person's residence for the day, simulating localized neighborhood mixing. If nobody eligible is within range, the agent simply stays home. There is no persistent social network or age-based mixing preference — every social visit is a fresh, spatially-local draw.

Why no explicit workplaces?

Adults do not travel to a modeled "work" location. Instead, income is treated as an intrinsic daily gain (§10) — a simplification that reflects how informal, agricultural, and home-based much of the local economy is, rather than attempting to model specific workplaces and commutes.

05

Disease transmission framework

Both pathogens are modeled using a shared compartmental structure — a modified SVEIR framework (Susceptible → Vaccinated → Exposed → Infectious → Recovered) — but each pathogen follows its own rules for how agents move between compartments. The two disease tracks run independently and simultaneously, so co-infection is possible: a child can be actively ill with both Rotavirus and Campylobacter at the same time. Both pathogens seed 1% of the population into the Exposed state at the very start of the simulation.

Fig 5.1 — Compartment transitions shared by both pathogens. Solid black = universal; blue = rotavirus-specific vaccination track; dashed purple = the care-seeking short-circuit available to either pathogen (§09).

Two kinds of immunity, kept separate

The model distinguishes two mechanistically different forms of acquired immunity, and it's worth keeping them apart:

Infection-risk immunity
A single global factor (\(\kappa=0.15\)) that reduces the probability of getting reinfected as an agent accumulates prior infections, decaying exponentially: \(\exp(-\kappa\cdot n_{\text{prior}})\). Shared by both pathogens; recovered individuals remain eligible for reinfection, just at reduced risk.
Symptom-severity immunity
A separate mechanism (§08) that reduces how sick a newly-infectious child becomes, based on vaccination and prior infection count — but only if the child gets infected in the first place. This is what makes repeat infections progressively milder even though they remain possible.
06

Rotavirus viral · contact + waterborne

Rotavirus spreads through three distinct opportunities for exposure each day, reflecting its dual nature as both a directly-transmitted and a water-transmitted pathogen. A vaccination track further modulates who is susceptible in the first place.

① Person-to-person
Direct transmission wherever susceptible and infectious agents are co-located — fires during both the day and night phases.
+
② At the water source
Infectious visitors can contaminate a water source; anyone drawing water from a contaminated source risks infection on the spot.
+
③ Brought home
A household member who fetched contaminated water can expose the rest of the household back home, even if they never left.

① Person-to-person transmission

infection probability given co-located contacts
\[ P_{\text{infect},i} = 1-(1-\pi_i)^{k} \]

where \(k\) is the number of currently infectious agents sharing agent \(i\)'s location — each is treated as an independent transmission opportunity — and \(\pi_i\) is agent \(i\)'s effective per-contact probability.

effective per-contact probability
\[ \pi_i = \beta_{rota}\times\exp(-\kappa\cdot N_{inf,i}) \]

The base rate is re-drawn stochastically every day, \(\beta_{rota}\sim\max\big(0.001,\ \mathcal{N}(0.0005,\,0.0002)\big)\), representing day-to-day variability in contact intensity. Vaccinated agents face a further breakthrough reduction of \((1-V_{eff})\); \(N_{inf,i}\) is the agent's lifetime infection count.

② Waterborne transmission — human ⇄ water ⇄ human

contaminating the source
\[ P(\text{clean}\rightarrow\text{contaminated}) = P_{shed} = 0.0001 \]

Rolled once per infectious agent who is physically standing at a water source that day.

getting infected from a contaminated source
\[ P_{\text{infect}} = P_{water\rightarrow human}\times m_i\times\exp(-\kappa N_{inf,i}) \]

\(P_{water\rightarrow human}=0.01\); applies to anyone standing at a contaminated source, with \(m_i=1-V_{eff}\) for vaccinated agents and \(m_i=1\) otherwise.

③ Household-carried contamination — night phase only

If the household's water-collector visited a contaminated source that day, every other household member is exposed once they're all back home — using the exact same infection probability as route ② above. This is how one family member's errand can put the whole household at risk without anyone else ever leaving the house.

Water source dynamics

Independently of human activity, each contaminated source recovers to clean with daily probability \(P_{recovery}=0.2\) (a mean recovery time of about five days). Separately, the whole grid faces a daily chance \(P_{shock}=1/30\) of a sudden, simultaneous contamination event across every currently clean source — representing acute events like heavy-rain runoff. Shocks are Rotavirus-relevant only, since Campylobacter's environmental route runs through animal density, not water.

Vaccination

Each day, every susceptible agent has a small chance (\(P_{vacc}=0.001\)) of becoming vaccinated. Vaccination does not grant full protection — it reduces the probability of infection across all three routes by a factor of \((1-V_{eff})\), with \(V_{eff}=0.55\), so breakthrough infections remain possible but less likely.

07

Campylobacter bacterial · zoonotic + fecal-oral + food-borne

Campylobacter spreads through three mechanistically distinct routes that operate independently each day: an environmental, animal-exposure route, a within-household fecal-oral route, and a flat background food-borne risk representing contaminated food eaten at home. There is no vaccination track for this pathogen.

1. Zoonotic route — household ownership → dose → Beta-Poisson infection

Every agent is exposed each day to a "dose" derived from the combined animal density at their current location — meaning this route fires wherever an agent happens to be, at home or away, both day and night. The density combines two species-specific layers (§02), each weighted by that species' relative contribution to zoonotic risk:

combined local density
\[ f_{combined}(x_i, y_i) = f_{poultry}(x_i, y_i)\times w_{poultry} + f_{ruminant}(x_i, y_i)\times w_{ruminant} \]

where \(w_{poultry}=1.0\) and \(w_{ruminant}=0.45\) reflect poultry's dominant role in C. jejuni source-attribution studies. Both density layers are built from household ownership draws at runtime (§02).

exposure dose
\[ D_i = f_{combined}(x_i,y_i)\times \rho_{campy} \]

where \(\rho_{campy}=0.25\) is the human–animal interaction rate.

Beta-Poisson dose-response infection probability
\[ P(\text{infect}\mid D) = \Big[1-{}_1F_1(\alpha,\ \alpha+\beta,\ -D)\Big]\times\exp(-\kappa\, N_{inf,i}) \]

\({}_1F_1\) is Kummer's confluent hypergeometric function; \(\alpha=0.038\), \(\beta=0.022\) are shape parameters. The dose is treated as a normalised exposure index rather than a literal organism count.

Fig 7.1 — Shape of the Beta‑Poisson dose‑response curve (schematic): risk rises steeply over a middle range of exposure and then saturates, so additional animal density beyond a point adds comparatively little marginal risk.

2. Fecal-oral household route — night phase only

trigger condition

Any household currently home for the night that contains at least one infectious member.

per-member infection probability
\[ P_{\text{infect}} = P_{fecal\rightarrow oral}\times\exp(-\kappa\, N_{inf,i}), \qquad P_{fecal\rightarrow oral}=0.03 \]

Applied once per day to every other member of an affected household, independent of exactly how many infectious members that household has.

3. Food-borne background route — once per day, location-independent

Campylobacter is also commonly picked up through contaminated food rather than direct animal contact. To capture this, every agent faces a small, flat daily infection chance that does not depend on where the agent is, the local animal-density layer, or the disease status of anyone else — it fires once per day for every agent, alongside disease progression, rather than during the twice-daily movement-linked transmission phases used by the other two routes.

flat daily background probability
\[ P_{\text{infect}} = P_{food}\times\exp(-\kappa\, N_{inf,i}), \qquad P_{food}=0.01 \]

Applied once per day to every susceptible-like agent (S or R), representing exposure via contaminated food consumed at home. The only individual factor that modulates it is the agent's own acquired immunity from prior infections — the same \(\exp(-\kappa N_{inf,i})\) decay term used throughout the model — so, unlike the zoonotic and fecal-oral routes, it carries no spatial or social signal at all.

Because all three routes are tracked separately, the model can report what share of all Campylobacter infections trace back to environmental (zoonotic) exposure, household (fecal-oral) spread, or the background food-borne route — a useful lens for comparing WASH-type interventions (which mainly affect the household route) against animal-management interventions (which affect the zoonotic route) and food-safety interventions (which affect the food-borne route). Recovered individuals remain eligible for reinfection through any of the three routes, at reduced risk, following the same immunity decay used throughout the model.

08

Illness severity, duration & health

Infection and clinical illness are treated as two different things. Only children under 5 are modeled as developing symptoms; adults are asymptomatic carriers — they can still be infected, still transmit, and still contaminate water, but they never accumulate a severity score or lose health from disease directly. Every child's illness is tracked independently per pathogen, so a co-infected child has two separate, concurrently-running episodes, each with its own severity and countdown.

Severity at onset

severity score, clamped to [0, 1]
\[ S_i = \text{clamp}\big(S_{base}\times M_{age}\times M_{immunity},\ 0,\ 1\big) \]

\(S_{base}\) is pathogen-specific: \(0.4\) for Rotavirus, \(0.3\) for Campylobacter, reflecting each pathogen's typical virulence.

age effect
\[ M_{age} = 1+1.5\exp(-0.08\times\text{age in months}) \]

Risk is highest immediately after birth and decays smoothly with age; adults are unaffected by this term since they never develop symptoms.

immunity effect
\[ M_{immunity} = \text{clamp}\big(1-I_{vaccine}-N_{inf}\times I_{prior},\ 0.1,\ 1.0\big) \]

\(I_{vaccine}=0.4\) applies only to vaccinated Rotavirus cases; \(I_{prior}=0.20\) per prior infection, for either pathogen. Even a maximally immune child retains a small residual severity.

How long an episode lasts

\[ D_{illness} = \text{round}\Big(D_{min}+S_i\times(D_{max}-D_{min})+\epsilon\Big), \qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\ \sigma_{duration}) \]

\(D_{min}=2\) days, \(D_{max}=12\) days, \(\sigma_{duration}=1.5\); the result is further clamped to \([D_{min},D_{max}]\). More severe onset means a longer expected illness, with individual variation layered on top.

While sick, and once well again

daily health toll (per active episode)
\[ h \leftarrow \max\big(0,\ h - S_i\times H_{-}\big), \qquad H_{-}=0.05 \]

If a child is fighting off both pathogens at once, the two health shocks are added together.

passive recovery (no active illness)
\[ h \leftarrow \min\big(1,\ h + R_{health}\times(1+w)\big), \qquad R_{health}=0.001 \]

Recovery is faster for wealthier households — reflecting better nutrition, rest, and living conditions — one of the model's clearest health↔wealth feedback channels alongside care-seeking.

An episode ends — severity and remaining duration both drop to zero — either when the countdown naturally reaches zero, or immediately, for both concurrent episodes at once, if the parent successfully seeks care (§09). This second pathway is what links the behavioral-economic layer directly back into epidemic dynamics: a successful clinic visit doesn't just make a child feel better, it also converts that child from Infectious to Recovered.

Passive recovery applies to any agent not currently experiencing an active illness episode — in practice this means it operates continuously for adults (who never develop clinical episodes) and for children in between bouts of sickness.
09

Care-seeking decisions Cumulative Prospect Theory

This is the model's central behavioral layer. Rather than assuming parents are perfectly rational cost-benefit calculators, SPRINGS-ABM gives every parent a Cumulative Prospect Theory (CPT) decision rule — capturing two well-documented cognitive biases: people weight losses more heavily than equivalent gains (loss aversion), and they distort probabilities, overweighting unlikely events and underweighting likely ones (probability weighting).

Noticing a sick child

Each day, for every child with a nonzero severity score, the parent "notices" the illness with a probability equal to that child's current worst severity across both pathogens — sicker children are more likely to be noticed. If more than one child in a household is sick, the parent focuses on whichever one appears sickest. If the household's wealth is below the cost of care, the parent is priced out and simply has to wait, regardless of what they would otherwise choose.

Fig 9.1 — The daily care-seeking decision funnel. The behavioral evaluation decides whether to seek care; a separate, objective chance draw then decides the outcome.

Utility & the reference point

extended Cobb-Douglas utility
\[ U(W,h_{\text{eff}}) = W^{\alpha}\,h_{\text{eff}}^{\,1-\alpha}, \qquad h_{\text{eff}} = (1-\omega)\,h_{\text{parent}} + \omega\,h_{\text{child}} \]

\(\alpha\) is the parent's personal wealth-versus-health weight (from their persona); \(\omega=0.5\) blends parent and child health equally. Crucially, the reference point for every comparison is today's actual state — the child currently sick — so a successful cure counts as a gain relative to right now, and this is exactly what makes loss aversion meaningful: parents feel the potential failure-loss more sharply than the recovery-gain.

The four possible outcomes

BranchObjective probabilityResulting stateDirection of change
Seek care → success\(P_{success}=0.80\)child health → full recovery, wealth −costgain
Seek care → failure\(1-0.80\)wealth −cost, parent stress, child unchangedloss
Wait → worsens\(P_{worsen}=0.35\)child severity +0.20, parent stressloss
Wait → stays the same\(1-0.35\)no change — this is the reference point0, by definition

Turning objective outcomes into subjective value

value function (asymmetric)
\[ v(\Delta) = \begin{cases} \Delta^{\theta} & \Delta \ge 0 \\ -\lambda\,(-\Delta)^{\eta} & \Delta < 0 \end{cases} \]

\(\theta=\eta=0.88\) (fixed, following Tversky & Kahneman); \(\lambda\in[1,3]\) is drawn per-agent — losses are felt \(\lambda\) times more strongly than an equally-sized gain.

Prelec probability weighting
\[ \pi(p) = \dfrac{p^{\gamma}}{\big(p^{\gamma}+(1-p)^{\gamma}\big)^{1/\gamma}} \]

\(\gamma\in[0.4,0.9]\), drawn per-agent; when \(\gamma<1\), small probabilities are overweighted and large ones underweighted (an inverse-S curve). This distortion only affects the parent's subjective evaluation — the world always resolves outcomes at the true, objective probability.

decision rule
\[ \underbrace{\pi(P_{success})\,v(\Delta U_{success}) + \pi(1-P_{success})\,v(\Delta U_{fail})}_{\text{seeking care}} \ \ \ge\ \ \underbrace{\pi(P_{worsen})\,v(\Delta U_{worsen})}_{\text{waiting}} \implies \textbf{seek care} \]

The "wait, and nothing changes" branch contributes \(v(0)=0\) and drops out of the comparison entirely.

Two simplified baselines, used to decompose the effect of CPT

Rational baseline (EV)
Same utility function and reference point, but objective probabilities and a linear value function — no probability distortion, no loss aversion. This is the textbook expected-value decision-maker.
Loss-aversion-only
Objective probabilities (no Prelec distortion), but the real, asymmetric, per-agent value function still applies — isolating the effect of loss aversion on its own, separate from probability weighting.

Comparing these three variants against each other (used in the calibration experiments, §12) shows how much of any deviation from "rational" care-seeking is driven by loss aversion versus by probability distortion.

10

Household economics

The economic layer is deliberately simple, but it's what closes the loop between illness and the ability to treat it.

daily income, per adult
\[ \text{income}_i = I_{base}\times h_i \quad\text{(health-scaled, by default)} \]
household wealth update
\[ W_{t+1} = \text{clamp}\Big(W_t + \sum_{\text{adults}} \text{income}_i \ -\ N_{members}\times C_{living},\ \ 0,\ 1\Big) \]

\(I_{base}=0.03\), \(C_{living}=0.025\) per household member per day. Only adults earn income; a cost of living is charged for every member, adult or child. The net change is applied to the household's single pooled wealth value, which is then shared back out equally to every member.

Because income scales with the earner's own health by default, illness doesn't just cost a household money directly through the price of care — it also reduces the income available to pay for that care in the first place. This creates a closed feedback loop: illness → lower income → less affordable care → illness persists or worsens → … — a dynamic that several of the model's calibration experiments (§12) are specifically designed to probe.

11

One simulated day, start to finish

Every mechanism described above is woven together once per simulated day, in a fixed sequence. The order matters: for instance, illness must be updated before care-seeking can act on today's severities, and transmission is evaluated twice — once wherever people are during the day, and once at home overnight — because each route depends on where agents physically are.

1 · Morning — disease progressesonce, before movement
  • For both pathogens: incubation clocks tick forward for exposed agents; those who have finished incubating become infectious; infectious agents face a stochastic daily chance of recovering.
  • For Rotavirus only: susceptible agents are offered vaccination at the daily vaccination rate, and that day's human-to-human infection probability is freshly redrawn.
  • For Campylobacter only: every susceptible agent faces the flat, location-independent food-borne background infection risk (§07) — applied once here rather than during the day/night transmission phases, since it has no spatial dependence.
2 · Day phase — activity & exposureagents at their chosen locations
  • Every agent samples one activity for the day from their personal time-use profile and travels to the corresponding location (or attempts a social visit) — §04.
  • Agents sharing a location are grouped together for the purposes of transmission.
  • Rotavirus: person-to-person transmission fires within each location group; anyone at a water source can contaminate it or become infected from it.
  • Campylobacter: the zoonotic route fires, driven by animal density at each agent's current cell.
3 · Night phase — home & household spreadeveryone back home
  • All agents return to their home location; location groups are recomputed — now equivalent to households.
  • Rotavirus: person-to-person transmission fires again, now at the household level.
  • Campylobacter: the zoonotic route fires again (evaluated at the home cell), and the fecal-oral household route becomes active for the first and only time each day.
4 · Daily systems — illness, behavior & economyfixed order, once per day
  • Illness update — new episodes begin for newly-infectious children, today's severity-driven health toll is applied, remaining duration counts down, and agents with no active illness recover passively.
  • Care-seeking — parents of noticed sick children weigh seeking care against waiting, using CPT (§09).
  • Household water carry-over — households whose water-collector visited a contaminated source today are exposed at home.
  • Environmental dynamics — contaminated water sources may recover; with a small daily chance, every clean source is contaminated at once (a shock event).
  • Household economics — daily income and cost of living settle into the household's pooled wealth (§10).
5 · Bookkeepingbefore the next day begins

The day's new infections and full compartment counts (susceptible, vaccinated, exposed, infectious, recovered) are tallied for each pathogen, and today's prevalence among children under 5 is appended to the running record — the core outcome measure used throughout the experiments in §12.

Experiments & calibration

Beyond a single run, the model is designed to support systematic parameter sweeps: the same simulation is repeated many times with a parameter varied and everything else held fixed, and outcomes are averaged over stochastic replicates. All outcome metrics are consistently restricted to children under 5, the population of primary interest.

One-at-a-time sensitivity analysis & calibration

A complementary sensitivity sweep perturbs four key transmission parameters one at a time around their baseline values — the Rotavirus contact rate, the water-to-human infection probability, the Campylobacter animal-interaction rate, and the Campylobacter fecal-oral probability — and compares the resulting episodes-per-child-year, peak prevalence, and zoonotic-route share against empirical target ranges drawn from sub-Saharan African and Ghanaian epidemiological literature. This provides a transparent way to check whether a given parameter setting produces plausible, literature-consistent disease burden before it is used for policy-relevant experiments.

Several parameters throughout the model — including the water contamination-shock and recovery rates, and the Beta-Poisson dose-response shape parameters — are currently informed assumptions rather than parameters calibrated directly against local data. The sensitivity analysis is an explicit first step toward that calibration, and the authors note plans to refine these against empirical water-quality testing, infrastructure-failure records, and regional time-use surveys.
